# Bilingual Chinese-English writing

## Purpose

Produce two texts that perform the same function for their readers. Do not force sentence-by-sentence symmetry when languages require different syntax or information order.

## Review priorities

1. Facts, numbers, names, citations and modal force match across languages.
2. Defined terms have a stable glossary.
3. Claims and qualifiers remain aligned.
4. Register matches the same institutional relationship.
5. Each version follows its locale and discourse community rather than importing the source language's directness, politeness, metaphor, or information order.
6. Headings and lists remain semantically parallel even when wording differs.

## Workflow

- Identify the authoritative source language.
- Create a term and protected-item table.
- Read the matching language pack and review each version independently in its own language, locale, genre, and institutional context.
- Run a cross-language semantic check after local polishing.

## Hard blocks

- silently resolving inconsistencies by choosing one side;
- weakening or strengthening obligations in translation;
- translating citations, names or policy titles without an authoritative form;
- inventing culturally specific anecdotes to make either version sound natural.
- treating US English, Mainland Chinese, or any other locale as a universal cultural default.
