# Professional English (`en`)

## Scope

Applies to general professional English. Confirm `en-US`, `en-GB`, another locale, or a publication-specific style whenever spelling, punctuation, dates, legal wording, or terminology matters.

## Review priorities

1. Preserve claim strength, evidence, qualifications, citations, and institutional roles.
2. Prefer explicit actors and actions when the genre allows, while retaining legitimate passive voice in methods, legal, and policy writing.
3. Match the relationship with the reader; direct language is not always appropriate, and formality is not automatically verbose.
4. Remove mechanical signposting, inflated significance, repetitive conclusions, and translation calques only when they do not serve the genre.
5. Keep terminology, spelling variant, capitalization, dates, numbers, and quotation conventions consistent with the target venue.

## Cultural and institutional guardrails

- Do not add contractions, idioms, jokes, anecdotes, or personal claims merely to sound native.
- Do not treat US business prose as universal English.
- Do not flatten discipline-specific hedging or legal modality into generic plain language.
- When adapting from Chinese, preserve function rather than sentence order; review the English version independently before cross-language alignment.
